title: "Getting distracted and Fahrenheit 451"
---

Ed's mind gets easily distracted.  It is curious and the internet provides with
all the information it needs and more.  It's kind of like humans liking sugars
and fats and fast food.

This makes it difficult to work or stay focused for long periods of time since
Ed's mind keeps asking questions:

- I wonder how you spell Fahrenheit?
- I wonder what are the prices for some of the newer Lenovo laptops?
- I wonder what are the prices for a Raspberry PI?

Ed's mind has also been (self) conditioned to consume content.  When it is
tired, it reacts by craving content.  Why does it do that?  A physical muscle
tears when being (over) used, but neurons simply transmit electrical impulses.

# Fahrenheit 451
I recently finished listening to Fahrenheit 451 by Ray Bradbury.  My mind thinks
the following about it.

- ✅ It was written a long time ago in 1954 and it is interesting to read it now
to see similarities between the predicted world and current one.
- ✅ Shorter attention span.  We seem to get bored more quickly and more quickly
move from one thing to another (forget)
- ✅ More consumption of content.  We have wireless headphones that can stay in
our ears for hours.  We listen to music, radio, podcasts and audiobooks when
running, cycling, walking, commuting, driving, falling asleep, doing house
chores, travelling, etc.
- ✅ Quantity not quality.  This is a general trend that can be seen in other
areas, especially music.  We have so much content that spending 20 hours reading
a book seems like a long time.  Thinking about it, your average 20-minute,
13-episode sitcom season takes 4h-5h.
- ❌ fortunately, I don't think book sales are dwindling.  Sure, we might have
audiobooks and e-readers, but it doesn't seem that people are losing their
interest in books.  Maybe it can still happen in the future?

# Remembering
Most humans don't seem to fully realise the implications / depth of this, but we
are the universe.  As Carl Sagan put it:

> The nitrogen in our DNA, the calcium in our teeth, the iron in our blood, the
> carbon in our apple pies were made in the interiors of collapsing stars. We
> are made of starstuff.\
> – _Carl Sagan_

DNA, fundamentally, is a way to remember things.  It's inefficient and clunky,
but it does it's job: it allows us to "remember" our evolutionary history.  To
remember the challenges we've faced as organisms thousands of years ago.
Fairly recently we've developed another, better way to remember: writing;
culture; books.  Before we had oral traditions, and those were good also.  They
served the same purpose.  Writing however is pretty much set in stone.  It
doesn't change.

As such, when you read: you are the universe being self-aware of itself.  Is
that me trying to give you new information, or is it me trying to help you
remember what you truly are, your true nature?
